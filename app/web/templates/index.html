STEP 1 · THE PICTURE

Spectrogram of the clip

A short sliding window turns the waveform into a time–frequency image — which frequencies sound, and when.

STEP 2 · THE FINGERPRINT

Constellation of peaks

Keep only the loudest local maxima. These sparse points survive noise and become the clip’s fingerprint.

STEP 3 · THE PROOF

The alignment spike

Every matched hash votes for a time offset (database frame − query frame). Chance matches scatter into a flat noise floor; a genuine match makes them converge on a single offset. That spike cannot be a coincidence.

DEEPER · WHY PAIR PEAKS?

Single peaks vs. paired hashes

A lone frequency peak recurs across countless songs, so single-peak matching is noisy. Pairing two peaks with their time gap into (f1, f2, Δt) is far rarer to collide on — the alignment spike sharpens dramatically. Compare them on this very clip:
